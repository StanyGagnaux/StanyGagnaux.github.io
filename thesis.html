Algorithmic strategies in hedge funds

Program
MSc Banking & Finance
Institution
ZHAW
Expected
2026
Field
Asset management
Abstract

How much of modern hedge fund strategy is actually automated, and where does automation stop paying off?

The increasing automation of investment processes is transforming asset management, particularly within hedge funds and other institutional investors. This thesis analyses which investment strategies (momentum, mean-reversion, arbitrage, and others) are currently used in practice, and to what extent they are automated.

A central focus is examining the frequency, contexts, and intensity of strategy deployment across different market conditions and hedge fund types. The study combines a literature review with empirical and secondary data analysis to identify patterns in the use of algorithmic and data-driven approaches.

It closes by evaluating the role and limitations of automation in contemporary financial markets, and what that implies for the future of asset management.

Research questions
  1. Which algorithmic investment strategies are most prevalent across hedge fund types, and how does their deployment vary by market regime?
  2. What market conditions (volatility, liquidity, regulation) drive the intensity of automation within specific strategy families?
  3. Where does automation measurably add value, and where do its limitations become binding?
Approach

The work is structured in two strands. A literature review establishes the conceptual framework and surveys existing evidence on algorithmic strategy adoption. An empirical analysis then works through secondary data (hedge fund databases, regulatory filings, industry reports) to assess deployment patterns across market conditions.

The goal is to close the gap between theoretical categorisation and observed practice, producing findings that are both grounded and useful.
